# A Manifesto on Human Authorship in a Digital Democracy

**Constitutional Thesis**  
Democratic governance in a digitally mediated society depends on preserving human authorship as a foundational condition of participation, ensuring that citizens can articulate, persist, and transmit intent with practical effect through the procedures and processes that constitute modern democracy.

## Preamble
Public authority, civic participation, and collective decision-making are now exercised primarily through digital systems: platforms, portals, consultations, standards, codes of practice, and procedural interfaces. These systems are not neutral conduits. They shape what can be expressed, how it is recorded, and whether it endures.

In this environment, citizens are formally invited to participate, yet rarely acknowledged as enduring authors of problems, intents, and resolutions. Inputs are collected, engagement is measured, and feedback is summarised, but authorship is often dissolved into aggregation. This document asserts that authorship remains central to democracy, even when exercised through highly mediated digital forms.

## 1. The Citizen as Author
Citizenship in the digital age is not defined by access to power, volume of participation, or proximity to decision-makers. It is defined by authorship: the capacity to identify a problem, to frame it with precision, and to sustain that framing across time and process.

Authorship does not imply authority over outcomes. It implies responsibility for intent. A citizen who authors a problem does not demand resolution on their own terms, but insists that the problem be recognised, preserved, and addressed without distortion. This strengthens representative democracy and institutional expertise by providing clarity at the point of origin.

## 2. Digital Mediation and Procedural Power
Digital governance systems define categories, constrain expression, impose timing, and privilege certain forms of input. Well-resourced actors adapt through continuity, specialised expertise, and informal access. Individual citizens encounter these systems episodically and without institutional memory. The resulting asymmetry is procedural: it favours actors who can maintain presence and repeat engagement. This manifesto recognises those effects and seeks to rebalance agency within them.

## 3. Precision Over Volume
In procedural environments, influence is not primarily a function of scale. Large volumes of input are easily summarised or abstracted. What persists is precision: clearly articulated intent, framed so institutions can carry it forward without reinterpretation. Precision allows a contribution to survive translation across administrative layers, time horizons, and personnel changes. It is the currency of durable participation.

## 4. Persistence as Democratic Practice
Democratic systems operate on extended timelines. Consultations close, initiatives evolve, and policies are reviewed years later. Organised actors sustain engagement; citizens often cannot. Authorship is meaningful only when it persists beyond the moment of submission.

## 5. Non-Ideological Engagement
This manifesto does not advance an ideology or prescribe policy outcomes. It concerns how participation is structured and remembered. By rejecting solution-first advocacy, it preserves the integrity of citizen intent while allowing institutions to act within their mandates. This restraint is discipline, not neutrality: durable influence aligns with institutional logic rather than confronts it.

## 6. Safeguarding Democratic Functioning
Democracy is safeguarded not only by formal rights but by the conditions under which those rights can be exercised effectively. When participation becomes symbolic, distorted, or ephemeral, legitimacy erodes quietly. Centering authorship, precision, and persistence safeguards democratic functioning in digitally mediated systems.

## Closing Assertion
This manifesto does not seek to oppose institutions, replace expertise, or equalise power where asymmetry is structural. Its purpose is to safeguard democratic functioning by establishing the conditions under which citizens can engage effectively within the procedures and processes that now constitute democratic governance. In systems shaped by organised influence and established access, agency is sustained through clarity of intent, disciplined expression, and continuity over time. What follows is resilience: the preservation of human authorship within procedural systems, ensuring that democratic participation retains practical effect as its forms become increasingly mediated.

## Immutability Statement
This manifesto is a fixed civic proclamation. It is not subject to revision, negotiation, or amendment in response to institutional feedback, political preference, or external authority. While it may be referenced, interpreted, or built upon through derivative works, its core assertions remain immutable. Any future evolution occurs through parallel or downstream contributions by citizens, not through alteration of this document. Its validity depends solely on whether citizens recognise it as truthful and choose to carry it forward.
